Lit Study

[jas]

2020/01/15

Tags: study article

I am meant to be writing some report on the literature in the field of
computer science, with specialization in programming language
theory. There are many things that I could potentially write on, but
the particularities of the subject are yet unclear (I should have
buckled-down on this two weeks ago, but it’s fiiine)

My supervisor presented me with a couple conferences (ICFP, PLDI)
wherein I should find some inspiration for papers to read and report
on. From sampling the different papers I found a few that sounded
interesting. My personal interest is in the facilities of
metaprogramming, how they can be used to construct new
(domain-specific) languages, and how these new languages are effective
in their goals.

A language is an open problem. Or maybe an open solution would be more
accurate. Languages in the context of programming will contain syntax
and semantics that are suited for their domain. C does well in
representing the machine at a level of abstraction just high enough to
be useful to write in; assembly also represents the machine but more
faithfully and thereby more laboriously (we make machines to do the
work for us, we made C to write assembly for us).

There are three fundamental parts to all programming languages.

  1. Primitives
  2. Combining Primitives
  3. Abstracting Combinations of Primitives

Programming languages must have primitives because they have to start
somewhere, natural languages have the questionable benefit of stewing
for thousands of years in human tongue imparting a long-storied
history every word and phrase in a multitude of ways. Wherein the
meaning of words have a layered history of etymological evolution.

Having a method for combining the primitives is crucial as well. This
just means that you can do something with them. For example, if you
take the integers as primitives, addition is one means of combination.

Then, you have the abstraction of the combination of primitives. This
is the trickiest part to understand, though the base concept is
simple, the consequences are expounding. That is, given integers and
addition define multiplication in terms of addition and you’ve got
yourself a new procedure in your little language!

(Note that in mathematics this would be a semigroup if you have a set
and an associative binary operator closed over the set! (then if you
add the identity element it is now a monoid!))

More on this later…
